1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
=============================================================

The technology sector, particularly the applied AI infrastructure
sub-segment, has continued to outperform broader equity indices
over the trailing twelve months, though dispersion within the
sector has widened materially. We see three distinct cohorts:
(a) compute and infrastructure providers, (b) enterprise software
companies integrating AI features, and (c) early-stage AI-native
firms with unproven unit economics. This note focuses primarily on
cohorts (a) and (b), as cohort (c) carries risk characteristics
generally unsuitable for Conservative or Balanced client profiles,
though we provide summary risk commentary on cohort (c) in Section
5 for completeness.

This expanded edition adds historical cycle comparison, supply
chain risk analysis, energy infrastructure constraints, and
illustrative bull/bear scenario framing to support more detailed
RM client conversations, while maintaining the same core house
view as the prior edition: constructive but selective.

2. MARKET CONTEXT AND MACRO BACKDROP
=============================================================

Global capital expenditure on AI-related data center buildout is
estimated to have grown substantially year-over-year, with
hyperscale cloud providers continuing multi-year infrastructure
commitments. Financing for this buildout has increasingly shifted
toward structured debt and joint-venture vehicles rather than pure
equity capex, which the research desk views as a signal of capital
discipline returning to the sector after a period of aggressive
balance-sheet expansion.

Broader macro conditions, as detailed in the Q2 2026 Global Market
Outlook (Document RN-001), remain a relevant backdrop: a more
balanced growth environment following the Q1 2026 rate volatility
episode provides a generally supportive, if not unambiguously
bullish, environment for risk assets including technology equities.
Interest rate sensitivity remains a relevant consideration for
long-duration growth equity valuations broadly, and technology
sector names — given their typically longer-duration cash flow
profiles — are more sensitive to discount rate assumptions than the
broader market.

3. COMPUTE & INFRASTRUCTURE COHORT — DETAILED ANALYSIS
=============================================================

Companies providing semiconductor design, fabrication capacity,
and data center hardware have benefited from sustained demand. Key
considerations for RMs discussing this cohort:

Revenue Concentration Risk: A small number of hyperscale customers
represent an outsized share of order books for several leading
firms in this cohort. This concentration means that a strategic
shift by even one or two major hyperscale buyers — whether due to
in-housing chip design, a shift to a competing supplier, or a
pullback in capex plans — could have an outsized impact on affected
suppliers' near-term revenue trajectories.

Capital Intensity: Fabrication capacity expansion requires
multi-year lead times, creating potential supply-demand mismatches.
The research desk notes that the current capacity expansion cycle,
if it proves larger than ultimately required by end demand, could
create a multi-year overhang on pricing power for commoditized
segments of the hardware stack, even as more specialized, harder-
to-replicate technology segments maintain pricing discipline.

Geopolitical Exposure: Export control regimes affecting advanced
semiconductor equipment remain a material risk factor for firms
with cross-border manufacturing dependencies. The desk continues to
monitor policy developments in this area closely, given the
potential for sudden regulatory shifts to materially affect
specific companies' addressable markets with limited advance
notice.

Capacity Utilization Trends: Leading-edge fabrication capacity has
generally run at high utilization rates, while legacy-node capacity
has shown more mixed utilization, reflecting bifurcated demand
between cutting-edge AI accelerator chips and more commoditized
semiconductor applications.

Power and Cooling Infrastructure: A growing share of capital
expenditure within this cohort is being directed toward power
delivery and cooling infrastructure rather than compute silicon
itself, reflecting the increasing recognition that power
availability, not chip supply alone, may become the binding
constraint on data center buildout over the medium term (see
Section 9 for further detail).

4. ENTERPRISE SOFTWARE COHORT — DETAILED ANALYSIS
=============================================================

Software companies embedding AI capabilities into existing
enterprise products have shown more moderate but steadier growth
than infrastructure pure-plays. Margin expansion from AI-feature
monetization has been uneven; several large vendors have reported
that AI-specific feature adoption has not yet translated into
proportionate pricing power.

Monetization Models Observed: The desk has observed three broad
monetization approaches across the cohort: (i) per-seat pricing
uplift for AI-enabled tiers, (ii) usage-based consumption pricing
tied to AI feature utilization, and (iii) bundled inclusion with no
direct incremental pricing, used by some vendors as a retention and
differentiation tool rather than a direct monetization lever.
Vendors using approach (ii) have generally shown the clearest
near-term revenue linkage to AI feature adoption, while vendors
using approach (iii) may be making a longer-term strategic bet on
retention and platform stickiness over immediate monetization.

Customer Adoption Patterns: Enterprise customers have shown
measured, rather than wholesale, adoption of AI-enabled features,
often piloting within specific departments or use cases before
broader rollout. This measured adoption pace has, in the desk's
view, been a contributing factor to the uneven pricing power
referenced above, as enterprise procurement processes continue to
demand demonstrated return on investment before committing to
broader AI feature licensing.

Competitive Dynamics: The cohort has seen increased competitive
intensity from both incumbent software vendors expanding into
AI-native feature sets and new entrants building AI-first
alternatives to established enterprise software categories. The
desk views incumbents' large existing customer bases and data
access as a meaningful, though not insurmountable, competitive
advantage over pure AI-native challengers in most enterprise
software categories.

5. AI-NATIVE / EARLY-STAGE COHORT — RISK COMMENTARY
=============================================================

This cohort comprises companies whose core business model is
built natively around AI capabilities, often with limited
operating history and, in many cases, not-yet-proven unit
economics at scale. While this cohort has attracted significant
investor attention and, in some cases, substantial valuation
premiums, the research desk views this cohort as carrying risk
characteristics generally unsuitable for Conservative or Balanced
client profiles, and only selectively appropriate for Growth or
Aggressive profiles with explicit concentration limit awareness.

Key risk considerations:

Unproven Unit Economics: Many companies in this cohort have not
yet demonstrated a clear path to sustainable profitability at
scale, with elevated compute costs relative to revenue in numerous
cases.

Customer Concentration and Churn: Early-stage AI-native companies
often exhibit higher customer concentration and, in some cases,
higher churn rates than more established enterprise software
peers, reflecting both the nascency of many use cases and intense
competitive dynamics within the cohort.

Capital Access Dependency: A meaningful share of this cohort
remains dependent on continued access to private or public capital
markets to fund operations, creating elevated sensitivity to shifts
in investor risk appetite.

Given these characteristics, the research desk does not currently
recommend direct single-name exposure to this cohort for any client
profile through the standard RM channel; any client interest in
this cohort should be evaluated through diversified vehicles where
available on the approved shelf, subject to the standard
suitability and concentration checks, or escalated per the
Suitability & Compliance Policy if no appropriate diversified
vehicle exists.

6. VALUATION OBSERVATIONS ACROSS COHORTS
=============================================================

Forward earnings multiples across the broader technology sector
remain above 10-year historical averages. The research desk's
internal view is that valuation dispersion, not absolute sector
valuation, is the primary risk consideration for portfolio
construction at this time. We do not publish single-stock price
targets in this note; single-name suitability must be checked
individually against the Suitability & Compliance Policy.

Across the three cohorts described above, the desk observes the
widest valuation dispersion, and correspondingly the widest range
of embedded growth expectations, within the AI-native cohort
(Section 5), followed by a moderate dispersion within the compute
and infrastructure cohort (Section 3), with the enterprise software
cohort (Section 4) showing comparatively more normalized valuation
ranges relative to historical sector averages, reflecting its more
established revenue base and more conventional monetization
trajectory.

7. HISTORICAL SECTOR CYCLE COMPARISON (ILLUSTRATIVE)
=============================================================

For RM context, the desk offers the following illustrative
historical framing, drawing on prior technology investment cycles.
This is provided for qualitative pattern context only and is
explicitly not a prediction that the current cycle will follow the
same trajectory as any prior cycle; markets, technologies, and
competitive dynamics differ across cycles in ways that limit the
predictive value of direct historical analogy.

Illustrative Pattern Observation A: Prior periods of significant
infrastructure-driven technology investment cycles have
historically exhibited an initial phase of broad-based investor
enthusiasm across many participants in the value chain, followed
by a maturation phase in which returns became more concentrated
among companies with durable competitive advantages, while
companies without such advantages saw valuation compression as
initial growth expectations were not met.

Illustrative Pattern Observation B: Enterprise software adoption
cycles for new general-purpose technologies have historically
shown a multi-year lag between initial product availability and
broad-based enterprise monetization, consistent with the measured
adoption pace currently observed in the enterprise software cohort
(Section 4).

The research desk's use of these illustrative historical patterns
is intended solely to provide RMs with qualitative framing for
client conversations about cycle dynamics, not to suggest specific
return expectations or timing for the current AI infrastructure
investment cycle.

8. SUPPLY CHAIN AND GEOPOLITICAL RISK ANALYSIS
=============================================================

The technology sector's supply chain, particularly for advanced
semiconductor manufacturing, remains geographically concentrated
in a small number of regions, creating structural supply chain
risk independent of company-specific execution. The desk monitors
several dimensions of this risk:

Manufacturing Geographic Concentration: A significant share of
advanced semiconductor fabrication capacity remains concentrated in
a small number of geographic regions, creating potential supply
disruption risk from geopolitical tensions, natural disasters, or
infrastructure disruptions affecting those regions.

Export Control Regime Evolution: Export control policies affecting
advanced semiconductor equipment and, in some cases, finished
products, continue to evolve across multiple jurisdictions. The
desk does not attempt to predict specific future policy actions but
notes that affected companies' addressable markets and supply chain
flexibility can shift materially with limited advance notice
following policy changes.

Diversification Efforts: Several major industry participants have
announced or begun executing geographic diversification strategies
for manufacturing capacity, though these efforts generally require
multi-year timelines to materially shift the geographic
concentration profile of the broader industry.

Critical Minerals and Materials: Certain materials critical to
semiconductor and battery manufacturing remain concentrated in
specific geographic supply sources, representing an additional,
often underappreciated, layer of supply chain risk beyond
manufacturing capacity itself.

9. ENERGY AND INFRASTRUCTURE CONSTRAINTS
=============================================================

Energy availability and grid capacity constraints in key data
center markets remain an underappreciated operational risk for the
compute and infrastructure cohort. The desk highlights several
sub-themes:

Grid Interconnection Timelines: In several key data center markets,
grid interconnection queue timelines have lengthened materially,
creating potential delays between data center construction
completion and full operational capacity, independent of chip
supply availability.

On-Site and Dedicated Power Generation: A growing number of large
data center operators have announced or pursued dedicated power
generation arrangements, including various low-carbon generation
sources, to manage grid constraint risk directly rather than
relying solely on grid interconnection, representing a meaningful
capital allocation shift within the cohort.

Water Usage Considerations: Data center cooling water usage has
become an increasing focus area for both regulators and local
communities in certain water-constrained regions, representing a
secondary but increasingly relevant operational and reputational
risk factor for data center operators and, by extension, their
hardware suppliers.

Regional Power Cost Variation: Regional variation in power costs
has become an increasingly important factor in data center site
selection decisions, with operators showing increased willingness
to site facilities in regions with favorable power cost and
availability profiles even where this requires longer network
latency to end users, reflecting the magnitude of power costs
within overall data center operating economics.

10. REGULATORY LANDSCAPE OVERVIEW
=============================================================

Regulatory scrutiny of large technology platforms continues to
evolve across multiple jurisdictions and could affect enterprise
software monetization models, as well as broader sector dynamics.
Relevant regulatory themes the desk monitors include:

Data Privacy and AI Governance Frameworks: Multiple jurisdictions
continue to develop or refine regulatory frameworks specifically
addressing AI model development, deployment, and governance,
creating potential compliance cost and operational complexity for
companies operating across multiple regulatory regimes.

Antitrust and Market Concentration Scrutiny: Regulatory scrutiny of
market concentration among large technology platforms continues in
multiple major jurisdictions, with potential implications for
business model flexibility, particularly for companies operating
significant platform or marketplace businesses alongside AI
infrastructure or software offerings.

Sector-Specific AI Regulation: Certain industry verticals,
particularly those involving healthcare, financial services, and
critical infrastructure applications of AI, face additional sector-
specific regulatory requirements that may affect the pace of AI
feature adoption within those verticals, relevant to the enterprise
software cohort's monetization trajectory in those specific
verticals.

The desk does not provide legal or regulatory compliance advice;
this overview is provided solely for general market context, and
RMs should not present specific regulatory predictions to clients
as confirmed outcomes.

11. PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION IMPLICATIONS
=============================================================

For RMs constructing or reviewing growth-oriented allocations:

- Diversified technology exposure via the Horizon Aggressive Equity
  Fund (HAEF) or Horizon Balanced Growth Fund (HBGF) sleeve
  allocations is generally preferable to concentrated single-name
  technology exposure for most client risk tiers.
- Clients with a Conservative risk profile should generally avoid
  direct sector concentration in technology beyond incidental
  exposure through diversified large-cap holdings.
- Clients with a Growth or Aggressive profile and appropriate time
  horizon may consider increased sector-tilted exposure, subject to
  standard concentration limits per the Suitability Policy (max 25%
  single-sector allocation absent documented client override).
- Given the valuation dispersion described in Section 6, the desk
  favors diversified exposure across all three cohorts over
  concentrated exposure to any single cohort, particularly the
  AI-native cohort described in Section 5.

13. RISKS TO THE THESIS — EXPANDED
=============================================================

- A pullback in AI-related infrastructure capex commitments by
  major cloud providers would disproportionately affect cohort (a)
  names, as described in Section 3.
- Regulatory scrutiny of large technology platforms continues to
  evolve across multiple jurisdictions and could affect enterprise
  software monetization models, as described in Section 10.
- Energy availability and grid capacity constraints in key data
  center markets remain an underappreciated operational risk, as
  detailed in Section 9.
- A faster-than-expected normalization of AI feature monetization
  expectations among enterprise software customers could pressure
  valuations across the enterprise software cohort if actual
  monetization continues to lag elevated investor expectations.
- Geopolitical or export control policy shifts could materially and
  rapidly affect addressable markets for companies with concentrated
  manufacturing or supply chain exposure, as described in Section 8.
- A broader market de-rating of long-duration growth equities,
  driven by a shift in interest rate expectations, would likely
  affect technology sector valuations disproportionately given the
  sector's generally longer-duration cash flow profiles.

14. BULL CASE AND BEAR CASE SCENARIOS (ILLUSTRATIVE)
=============================================================

The following illustrative scenarios are provided for RM
preparation and qualitative client conversation framing only. They
are not price targets, return forecasts, or investment
recommendations, and must not be presented to clients as such.

Illustrative Bull Case: Continued sustained capital expenditure
commitments from hyperscale cloud providers, combined with broader-
than-currently-expected enterprise adoption of AI-enabled software
features translating into proportionate pricing power, supports
continued above-market growth across the technology sector, with
valuation premiums sustained by durable earnings growth rather than
multiple expansion alone.

Illustrative Bear Case: A combination of capex discipline among
major hyperscale buyers, continued lag in enterprise AI feature
monetization, and a broader market de-rating of long-duration
growth equities driven by shifting rate expectations leads to a
period of valuation compression across the sector, with the
greatest impact concentrated in the AI-native cohort (Section 5)
and more speculative segments of the compute and infrastructure
cohort.

Illustrative Base Case (Desk's Central View): A continuation of the
current bifurcated pattern, with durable competitive advantages
increasingly differentiating performance across all three cohorts,
supporting the desk's preference for diversified rather than
concentrated sector exposure as described in Section 11.

16. GLOSSARY OF SECTOR-SPECIFIC TERMS
=============================================================

Hyperscale Cloud Provider: A company operating cloud computing
infrastructure at very large scale, typically with global data
center footprints supporting both internal and third-party
computing workloads.

Compute and Infrastructure Cohort: Companies whose primary business
involves designing, manufacturing, or operating the physical and
semiconductor infrastructure underlying AI workloads.

AI-Native Company: A company whose core product or service was
designed from inception around AI capabilities, as distinct from an
established company adding AI features to an existing product.

Capex (Capital Expenditure): Spending on physical assets such as
data centers, equipment, and infrastructure, as distinct from
operating expenses.

Fabrication Capacity: The physical manufacturing capacity available
to produce semiconductor components, often a key supply-side
constraint in the compute and infrastructure cohort.

Grid Interconnection Queue: The administrative and technical
process by which a new electricity demand source, such as a data
center, is connected to the regional power grid, which can involve
significant timeline uncertainty in constrained regions.

17. APPENDIX: RM PRE-APPROVED DISCUSSION FRAMEWORK
=============================================================

For RMs preparing to discuss technology sector positioning with
clients, the following structured discussion framework is provided
to ensure conversations remain grounded in the client's documented
risk profile and suitability constraints, consistent with the
Suitability & Compliance Policy.

Step 1 — Confirm Current Risk Profile and Time Horizon: Before
discussing any sector-specific positioning, confirm the client's
current risk profile tier is on file and not expired, and confirm
the relevant time horizon for the portion of the portfolio under
discussion, per the Client Risk Profiling Methodology (Document
CMP-003).

Step 2 — Establish Existing Sector Exposure: Calculate the client's
current aggregate technology sector exposure across all holdings,
including direct single-name positions and fund-level exposure from
products such as HAEF and HBGF, before discussing any incremental
allocation.

Step 3 — Frame the Cohort Distinction: Where relevant to the
client's question or interest, use the three-cohort framework from
this note (compute and infrastructure, enterprise software,
AI-native) to provide qualitative risk differentiation, without
referencing specific company names outside the approved product
shelf.

Step 4 — Apply Concentration Limits: Confirm that any proposed
incremental allocation does not breach the 25% single-sector
concentration limit (Restricted List Rule-002) without a documented
and approved override.

Step 5 — Document and Cite: Ensure the resulting ClientBrief
includes appropriate citation to this research note and the
relevant product guide(s), consistent with the firm's grounding and
citation requirements for all client-facing recommendations.

Step 6 — Apply Compliance Gate: Route the resulting recommendation
through the standard compliance gate before client delivery,
consistent with Section 3 of the Suitability & Compliance Policy.

RMs should treat this framework as a structured checklist rather
than a script; client conversations should remain natural and
responsive to the client's actual questions and concerns, with this
framework serving as a backstop to ensure suitability and compliance
requirements are not inadvertently skipped during a fluid
conversation.

Step 7 — Revisit Periodically: Sector positioning discussions should
be revisited at each scheduled portfolio review, not treated as a
one-time conversation, given the pace of change described throughout
this note across the compute, software, and early-stage cohorts.
Material developments in any of the risk areas described in Sections
8 through 10 may warrant an off-cycle conversation if the RM judges
the development sufficiently relevant to the client's existing
holdings.
